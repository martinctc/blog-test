Two Styles of Learning R

What‚Äôs the best way to learn R?

Motivations behind the debate

Some argue that R fundamentally has a steep learning curve, and that there are no real shortcuts for learning R. I don‚Äôt completely agree with that: I think that there are easier ways to learn R nowadays, specifically with the availability and expansion of the tidyverse collection of packages. From my own experience, and from chatting with other people about their experiences in self-teaching R, I think that learning approaches for R (especially at the ‚Äúnewbie‚Äù stage) fall into either one of two styles: the ‚Äòanarchic‚Äô style and the ‚Äòmethodical‚Äô style. There‚Äôs a valuable debate to be had on which approach/style is better, and I‚Äôm sure someone from the #rstats community has discussed this at some point. Nonetheless, I do feel that it‚Äôs worth outlining this question as it is quite an important one, as making the learning of R accessible to a wide audience is key to the thriving of the R community, its artefacts (e.g.¬†packages, analysis outputs), and the language itself. If you want more people to use R, you have to make it easier for them to learn R!

The ‚ÄòAnarchic‚Äô Style ü§î

The first style is what I (rather dramatically) refer to as the ‚Äòanarchic‚Äô style, or the ‚Äògreedy‚Äô style; many R users I know and certainly myself fall into this camp. By an anarchic learning style, I am referring to an ‚Äúanything goes‚Äù style of learning that follows no rule or principle in selecting the source and method for learning R. For example, this means you don‚Äôt simply stick to a particular book or a tutorial, but rather absorb knowledge about R indiscriminately through whatever source you come across, including:

  • Stack Overflow discussions
  • RPubs documents / vignettes on GitHub
  • R blogs on R-bloggers or R Weekly
  • YouTube videos
  • Structured tutorial videos on DataCamp / Udemy, etc.

Most people who are learning R on their own without a mentor would probably naturally lean towards this style, as the lack of guidance means that there is especially a need to explore different mediums and methods to find out what works best for you.

What‚Äôs characteristic about this style is that it is knowledge gap-driven and method-agnostic: you focus on what your knowledge gaps are, and explore methods indiscriminately with the sole aim of filling the gap. For instance, I might be a beginner in R with no background in natural language processing or text mining, and decide that I want to learn to run some basic text analysis in R for some Twitter posts I got my hands on. Instead of identifying a single good book or tutorial that introduces you to the basic concepts and operations such as n-grams, word-stemming and creating document-term matrices, as an ‚Äòanarchic‚Äô learner I would scour the Internet for any blogs, Stack Overflow discussions, vignettes and videos that contain the terms ‚Äútext mining‚Äù, ‚Äúanalysis‚Äù, and ‚Äúin R‚Äù, and try to make sense of all the information that I come across.

If the above is a somewhat representative description of your learning style, you‚Äôre probably well aware of its advantages and disadvantages. On the one hand, you are likely to end up with a very comprehensive view of what‚Äôs out there - along the way, you‚Äôll probably learn how to download books through R (see gutenbergr) and create interactive wordclouds (see wordcloud2), even if you weren‚Äôt necessarily looking out for how to do these things in the first place! You‚Äôll realise that there are at least two major R packages available out there for text mining (tm and tidytext), and if you‚Äôve spent enough time learning, you‚Äôll probably also have figured out two or more different ways of doing pretty much the same thing.

All sounds great, but on the other hand this style of learning is painfully inefficient: if your ultimate goal is to be able to run a word frequency analysis, why would you need to learn two ways of doing it? Whilst these may serve as valuable lessons in the long term, isn‚Äôt it a poor use of time to trawl through and experiment with the ‚Äògood‚Äô and ‚Äòless good‚Äô methods that other users have come up with, in order to figure out how to do this yourself? The inefficiency of this approach also makes it much more likely that someone will give up on learning, especially if this is somebody who is learning on the job or isn‚Äôt learning R full-time.

The problem of inefficiency partly lies with the diverse nature of R‚Äôs package eco-system: packages are largely written by individual contributors and developers, not by a single team of developers sitting in the same office. Since this is a distributed process, it‚Äôs almost inevitable that there will be duplications, or very similar methods to achieve the same thing in R (just think about how many ways you can filter rows in R). I wouldn‚Äôt change this about R at all, as I think this is part of what makes R such a great language; however, this does pose a very material obstacle for new comers to R. As someone who learnt R through the ‚Äòanarchic‚Äô approach, there were many times that I felt frustrated simply because the available learning resources had inconsistent syntax styles (e.g.¬†dplyr vs data.table vs base), and so much time was spent simply trying to figure out what I really needed to know in order to do the thing that I wanted to do.

The ‚ÄòMethodical‚Äô Style

So here‚Äôs the alternative - the ‚ÄòMethodical‚Äô Style - which is simply the opposite of the ‚ÄòAnarchic‚Äô approach.

Instead of ‚Äògreedily‚Äô consuming all the information that is available and accessible, this style applies a much stricter discipline in selecting sources of information. For instance, one might choose to learn and stick to R for Data Science as the core textbook to go through, rather than dipping into older textbooks that might use a mixture of base R code and possibly reference bits of dplyr syntax. The idea is that this more disciplined style works well to quickly onboard new learners on how to run analysis in R using the more intuitive tidyverse style, and only at a later stage expose the learners to more complex concepts and syntax in R once they have greater familiarity with the language. The argument is also that since new learners can much quickly achieve some ‚Äòeasy wins‚Äô and are less likely to get confused with this approach, the drop-off rate (i.e.¬†giving up) at the early stages will be lower and hence are much more likely to convert into fluent R users.

Which style is better?

When I first started learning R (~ 2014), tidyverse wasn‚Äôt quite as developed or well-known, and the only kind of methodical learning approach available was to learn base R - which wasn‚Äôt quite as intuitive or accessible for a beginner. Today, tidyverse presents a clearly better alternative. My view is that new learners who have zero or very limited experience of programming should apply the ‚ÄòMethodical‚Äô style and learn the tidyverse syntax, and only switch to a more ‚Äògreedy‚Äô style when they start to get to grips with functional programming or more advanced applications of R. The ‚Äòanarchic‚Äô / ‚Äògreedy‚Äô style is ultimately important because it enables you to take a comprehensive view of the solutions which are out there in the R universe, and this comprehensive view is essential for coming up with creative and tailored solutions to real-life analytical problems.1
